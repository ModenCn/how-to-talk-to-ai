## ①

**Original thesis:** The principle of vibe coding is this: out of all the computer programs this world could possibly hold, vibe coding uses AI to rapidly *converge* onto one specific possibility.

**Expansion:**

Imagine an abstract space: all the computer programs that could possibly exist form a near-infinite "space of possibilities." Each point in it is a concrete piece of software—it might be a working app, or it might be a heap of gibberish that will never compile. The vast majority of points are meaningless; only a tiny fraction correspond to programs that are genuinely useful, that actually solve some problem.

Traditional programming is a human **walking through this space step by step**: writing a line of code is nudging one small step in some direction; debugging and refactoring are constant course corrections. By experience and logic, the programmer hand-builds the system from "nothing at all" to "that one specific possibility." The process is slow, craft-dependent, and hinges entirely on the human's grip on the path.

What vibe coding changes is **the way you arrive**. You no longer walk step by step; instead you describe to the AI the "feel" (the vibe) of what you want—the goal, the constraints, the style, the intent. Drawing on the priors it has learned from oceans of code, the AI treats this blurry intent as a kind of gravity, and **collapses the entire space of possibilities, almost instantly**, onto one specific implementation. The clearer your description, the stronger the pull, the more precise the landing.

One line for the principle:

> It isn't teaching the AI to write code; it's using AI to collapse "all the shapes a computer could take" into "the one shape you want right now."

**Note · Contrast with the "Infinite Monkey Theorem":**

The Infinite Monkey Theorem: a monkey hitting keys at random, given infinite time, will almost surely (probability 1) type out any given text—say, the complete works of Shakespeare. The principle: all character sequences form a space, and a random process, given infinite time, will eventually traverse any finite sequence in it. But that probability is astronomically small; the time required dwarfs the age of the universe.

It is the exact mirror image of vibe coding—the same "space of possibilities," two ways to arrive:

| | The monkey | vibe coding |
|---|---|---|
| Same thing | space of possibilities | space of possibilities |
| How it finds | pure random search | AI directed convergence |
| Relies on | luck | priors + your intent (gravity) |
| Cost | infinite time | nearly instant |

In one line: what the AI does is **compress the "infinite time" the monkey would need into a single prompt**.

# Chapter 1: Why Humans Are Indispensable in vibe coding

## Why artificial intelligence cannot replace humans

**Core claim:** AI cannot fundamentally replace humans, and the deepest reason is analogous to why quantum mechanics needs an "observer."

**Argument:** The hypothetical "observer" in quantum mechanics must be triggered by some subject. Even if that subject is non-volitional, there must still be a volitional subject that actively triggers it; and even where a non-volitional subject indirectly triggers an observation, that is merely a necessity produced by possibility itself—that is to say, if a volitional subject uses any indirect means and thereby causes this collapse, then traced to its root it is still the volitional subject interfering with possibility, making possibility land within some fixed interval.

In this sense, **humans can choose the future**.

I call this act **"Will-Collapse"**—the will intervenes in causation, making one out of countless possibilities land as reality.

**Corollary:** So the human will (or subjectivity) is a thing of immense power—because it can decide the direction in which real things fall.

**Limit (tension):** But humans are also bound by the "light cone"—possibility has a range, and one cannot leap outside that range. Moreover, these possibilities, like water flowing downhill, naturally fall toward some direction; the human will is **resisting** this natural descent, and so necessarily meets resistance.

**The point:** This, then, is the shape of fate.

**Back to vibe coding (conclusion):** So vibe coding, as a thing, cannot be separated from the human—every convergence it makes is, in essence, an act of **Will-Collapse**.

# Chapter 2: The Essence of vibe coding

**Opening:** vibe coding evolves with the times—that is, as AI grows more capable, the form of vibe coding keeps changing.

**Thought experiment (the limit case):** Imagine an extreme case—any thought of yours can be copied, 100%, into "the possibility-form of a program."

That is to say: **the program = the complete body of your thought**.

**The real-world deviation:** But in reality, your communication with the AI is only an "exchange of data"—the AI cannot directly copy out the result in your head, so deviation is inevitable.

**One layer deeper (the source is blurry too):** And likewise, the result in your own head also carries deviation—because the human brain, as a neural network, is not a deterministic product; it "blurs," just as an AI-generated image always has some spot that's fuzzy.

**The two faces of abstraction:** The brain's "abstraction mechanism" gives it efficient computation; but at the same time, it needs something to constantly calibrate it.

**The calibrator = the real world (the reference frame):** The reason the human brain cannot fully leave "the real world as its anchor" is precisely this—a neural network needs a definite reference standard (a reference frame) to calibrate itself against.

**The reference frame must be stable:** And this "standard" cannot drift. Once the reference standard itself drifts, the neural network loses its anchor and falls into disorder.

**Especially fatal for self-recursive systems:** Especially for a system that can "self-recur," this drift of the reference is fatal.

**Real vs. simulated intelligence (a criterion):** No matter how powerful an AI becomes, as long as it needs a "root cause"—needs a human to push the first step of its behavior, and cannot generate its own motive—then it cannot be regarded as possessing real intelligence; it can only be said to "simulate" intelligence.

**The correct form (conclusion):** So the true form of vibe coding should be—oriented by a **concrete goal**, with a **coupled collaboration** formed between AI and human.

**The human's fundamental role in the coupling:** And the reason the human must take part in this coupling step is precisely this—to eliminate the deviation between **result** and **goal**.

**The essence:** This, then, is the essence of vibe coding—**continuously eliminating the deviation between "result" and "goal."**

# Chapter 3: Why You Should Not Look at the Code

## I. The coupling of complex systems, and design orientation

**The claim:** From the angle of "the coupling of complex systems" and "design orientation"—why you should not look at the concrete code implementation.

Let's argue it through a simple thought experiment:

**The setup:**
- **Party A** = the initiator of the project
- **Party B** = the executor of the project

**The scenario:** You (Party A) are the source of the project's requirements; you spent a lot of money and hired Party B to implement it.

**The core:** You (Party A) don't need to know any code implementation—you only know "what you want"; all the concrete implementation is done by Party B.

**The process:** Then, through many rounds of communication, the two of you shape the project into the concrete deliverable you want.

**The distillation:** Throughout this process, Party A needs to read not a single line of concrete code. That is to say—between A and B there exists some "abstraction mechanism."

**The corollary:** And this layer of abstraction mechanism can be simulated by a neural network—so AI will inevitably replace this middle abstraction layer.

**Key concept:** The important concept here is—if you look at the code, you have in fact stepped out of your identity as Party A.

**The three-layer model:** The logic now is—**A — [abstraction layer B] — code C**:
- **A** = you (the initiator)
- **B** = the abstraction layer, i.e. the earlier Party B (the AI executor)—what the executor carries is precisely this layer of abstraction
- **C** = the code (the final implementation)

When you bypass the abstraction layer (B) to look at the code (C), it works against your using AI in a "fully abstracted form"—because what you need is the **ability to communicate in the abstract**.

**The methodology:** You should not, in any form, look directly at the code (C). You should look at the **effect (D)**, and then communicate with the **abstraction layer B**.

**Real-world corroboration:** In fact, in the real world the client never looks at the code either.

**Closing (back to "coupling"):** So the reason you shouldn't look at the code is precisely the root reason you need "coupling"—you need to couple the **goal** based on the **result**. If you go straight to the implementation layer, that amounts to taking over the work of the middle abstraction layer.

# Chapter 4: Who Is Responsible for the Code

**Opening (the real worry):** Often you worry that AI-written code, dropped into a real project, will go wrong.

**The fact:** But in fact, it isn't only AI-written code that goes wrong—human-written code goes wrong just the same.

**Key claim:** A mature software company solves these latent problems through **process**, not through some particular person.

**Emphasis:** Humans make mistakes, AI makes mistakes—and that is no problem at all.

**Reality:** The client wants a program that never errs, but in reality that does not exist.

**The truly feasible form:** What truly exists in reality is *lowering the severity of the consequences when an error occurs*.

**The core:** We know the system will err—but we know what the **maximum severity** is when it does.

**Conclusion (three lines of defense):** A testing process, a sandbox environment, backup redundancy—these three are the real foundation for getting a project to production, not an AI that never errs.

# Chapter 5: Why Traditional Programming Will Be Phased Out

**Opening (the core):** Efficiency is the key to everything.

**Argument (efficiency → productivity → value):** We live in a high-efficiency society—efficiency determines productivity, and productivity determines value.

**The assertion:** Code in itself creates no value. **Code in itself is just code—code is just stone.**

**The source of value:** Code produces value only once it is used, once it takes part in human action.

**Code vs. art (equal and opposite):** Code and art are almost "equal and opposite" terms—art has value in itself; whereas code has value only in its "efficient implementation," and the value of ineffective code is approximately 0.

**The essence of traditional programming:** So all the means, methods, types, and abstract objects of traditional programming—these operations exist only to let humans (better) stand in for that middle abstraction layer. In reality, the machine only needs to execute some definite instruction.

**The climactic corollary:** Since a human can stand in for the middle abstraction layer, it means a neural network can stand in for the abstraction layer. So AI does not need the middle process—AI can directly collapse your requirement into some definite state of the system. This is destined to happen one day.

**Why we still need code now:** And the reason we still need programming languages and code today is, in essence—humans need to audit the AI, to inspect and review it.

**Analogy (the safety valve):** It's like how all safety-critical production has both an "automatic" and a "manual" switch—the manual one is the last safety valve kept for any critical moment.

**Advancing:** And as the times change, AI's auditing efficiency will far exceed humans'—so even auditing, testing, and verification should be handed to AI.

(There's a hidden risk here: "AI auditing AI" runs into Chapter 2's "drift of the reference is fatal for self-recursive systems"—if both execution and audit are the same AI, who provides the stable external reference? The resolution is in Chapter 6.)

# Chapter 6: The Limits of AI and the Diversity of Humans

(working title)

**The problem (AI's fixed meme vs. human diversity):** But in reality there is a problem—AI has a certain "fixed meme," whereas humans are diverse.

**The claim:** AI's thinking, however comprehensive, cannot cover every situation.

(The auditing referred to here is *technical* auditing, not *result* auditing.)

AI has its own personality; every AI model differs in personality and traits.

When AI is trained as a tool, it tends to output the same result. If it gave a different result every time—say I tell it "the apple is red" and it names a different color each time—that would mean the system is a chaotic system.

So, opposite to its chaos, the same AI has a fixed bias toward the same thing.

Different AIs have different personalities.

So the same project should be verified repeatedly by AI models of different personalities, to lower the probability of error.

# Chapter 7: The Death of C++

C++ uses complex interface types and template classes to lower the difficulty of development in real programming and to raise system efficiency.

But C++ exists to solve the problems humans run into when collaborating. AI does not need this means to solve problems—that is to say, AI can achieve everything C++ does through a complete "C-ification."

And the vast majority of complex projects, to ensure usability, are built through abstraction mechanisms, so as to keep errors from causing substantive harm. That is to say, the whole trade of "using a low-level language for complex implementation, trading low development efficiency for high runtime efficiency" has already become meaningless.

So C++, as a language, is destined to perish in the long river of history. And the language that becomes mainstream next will be determined by three things together: extremely high adaptability, extremely strong generality, and AI's low error rate on it.

# Chapter 8: The Closed Loop of the AI System

Any open-loop behavior in the AI's execution process should be turned into closed-loop behavior.

Because open-loop behavior blocks the AI's ability to fix problems within its current capability, whereas a closed loop strengthens that ability—it gives it a reference baseline from which it can revise.

So in the development process, you should turn the open-loop system into a closed-loop system, giving the AI feedback.

This is another reason you shouldn't bypass the abstraction layer to modify the code—if you change it, the AI doesn't know, and this indirectly hurts the AI's efficiency in its subsequent execution.

# Chapter 9: The Attention Mechanism, and Notes

In weaker models, attention has to be maintained by hand. And notes are a method for raising the AI's attention on certain things.

In stronger models, notes and attention-maintenance can ensure its consistency of implementation across a project.

# Chapter 10: Prompt Pollution

If a model is strong enough, you should describe as little as possible—your description will limit the AI from exercising its own ability.

The meaning: between you and the AI, there is always one who understands the matter more deeply. But if the one who understands more deeply is the AI, then describing it through your own understanding will actually lower the chance that it reaches your goal by a better method.

So the weaker the AI, the more concretely you should describe—to keep it from continuing down its own error (steering the result to collapse toward the right direction); and the stronger the AI, the more you should let it land on a better direction by itself—the more you say, the more likely you steer it onto a suboptimal solution.
